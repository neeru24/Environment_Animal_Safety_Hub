Saving Our Oceans:
The Microplastic Problem

Discover the invisible threat choking our seas and learn how we can turn the tide on plastic pollution before it's too late.

Microplastics are now found in every ocean, from surface to seafloor.

What Are Microplastics?

Microplastics are plastic particles smaller than 5mm – smaller than a sesame seed. They're so tiny they can travel through water filtration systems and enter our food chain.

We're filling the ocean with plastic confetti that never goes away – it just breaks into smaller and smaller pieces that enter every level of marine life.

Understanding the Scale

Microplastics come in different sizes, each with unique impacts on marine life.

Microplastics
(< 5mm)

Invisible to the naked eye but everywhere in our oceans.

  • Smaller than a sesame seed
  • Pass through water filters
  • Ingested by plankton
  • Enter food chain at base
  • Most abundant in ocean

Example: Microbeads from cosmetics, synthetic fibers from clothing

Mesoplastics
(5mm - 2.5cm)

Visible plastic fragments that are breaking down but not yet microscopic.

  • Size of a pencil eraser
  • Visible to naked eye
  • Ingested by small fish
  • Often mistaken for food
  • Continues to break down

Example: Broken bottle fragments, degraded packaging pieces

Macroplastics
(> 2.5cm)

Large plastic items that will eventually break into microplastics.

  • Larger than a bottle cap
  • Entangles marine life
  • Blocks digestive tracts
  • Source of microplastics
  • Most visible pollution

Example: Plastic bags, bottles, fishing nets, containers

Where Do They Come From?

Microplastics enter our oceans from many surprising sources – not just litter.

35%

Synthetic Clothing

A single laundry load can release 700,000 microfibers. These tiny plastic fibers pass through water treatment plants into rivers and oceans.

28%

Tire Wear

As tires wear down on roads, they release microplastic particles that wash into waterways with rainwater. One of the largest sources of microplastics.

24%

City Dust

From artificial turf, paint flakes, and construction materials. Urban areas generate significant microplastic dust that enters stormwater systems.

13%

Road Markings

Plastic-based road paint wears off and becomes microplastic particles. These wash into drains and eventually reach the ocean.

Impact on Marine Life

The devastating effects of microplastics throughout the ocean ecosystem.

Bioaccumulation

Plastic builds up in animals' bodies, increasing in concentration up the food chain.

Human Consumption

We consume up to a credit card's worth of plastic each week through seafood and water.

Toxic Chemicals

Plastics absorb pollutants like PCBs, which then enter marine organisms when ingested.

Coral Damage

Microplastics can smother coral reefs and block sunlight needed for photosynthesis.

Solutions in Action

From personal choices to global initiatives, here's how we're fighting back.

Advanced Filtration

New washing machine filters can catch 90% of microfibers before they enter wastewater systems.

Circular Economy

Redesigning products to eliminate waste and keep materials in use reduces plastic pollution at source.

Biodegradable Alternatives

Developing plastics that safely break down in marine environments without creating microplastics.

Ocean Cleanup Tech

Innovative systems that capture plastic from rivers before it reaches the open ocean.

What You Can Do

Simple actions that make a big difference in reducing microplastic pollution.

Choose Natural Fibers

Opt for clothing made of cotton, wool, linen, or hemp instead of synthetic materials.

Use a Guppyfriend Bag

Wash synthetic clothes in special bags that catch microfibers before they enter waterways.

Avoid Microbeads

Check personal care products and avoid those with "polyethylene" or "polypropylene" in ingredients.

Support Cleanup Efforts

Join beach cleanups or support organizations working on ocean plastic solutions.

Protect Our Blue Planet

The ocean gives us life, food, and oxygen. It's time to return the favor. Together, we can turn the tide on microplastic pollution.

Every action counts in the fight to save our oceans.
